### Landscape Scan
When observing various current digital editions, one key aspect is abundantly clear: the creators of these editions do not have the time, and often do not have the skill, to create a fully functional and well-designed interface.  Most of the projects are rich with scholarship, but do not have a fundamental understanding of how to present their work.  These digital editions are often so focused on the scholarship side of development that not only is the website design left as an afterthought, but so is the user.  Many of the projects are frustrating to use, as they seem to be designed for the people who created them, and not the intended user.

Many digital editions still rely on adapting design from static or print editions rather than taking advantage of the digital medium to create an entirely new textual experience.  There is a lack of innovation that utilizes the digital in order to create an entirely new way of interacting and experiencing the text that only a digital edition could offer.  The design itself, from font choice to color scheme, is more indicative of a website from the early 2000s than a current scholarly source.  This presents a major issue when it comes to drawing in outside readers and users.  A website that appears outdated, both in terms of design and interface, often implies a lack of credibility and security.  Images, if there are any, are often either too small to be usable, or not well integrated.  Text is sometimes too small to be legible, making for an uncomfortable reading experience.  Notes and glosses are often placed in odd locations that make them more difficult for the reader to use.  The following are examples of current digital editions available for viewing online.

#### 1.1 The University of Rochester’s TEAMS
This digital edition of The Canterbury Interlude and the Merchant’s Tale of Beryn (Figure 1.1.1) highlights this issue of implementing design inspired by print editions.  The layout of the Middle English text on the left, with its accompanying line numbers, and the glosses on the right are all visually identical to a print edition.  The main issue here is the amount of space between the text and the glosses.  While design like this mostly works in print form, the text is so small, and the glosses so far away, that it is easy for a reader to get lost on their way from word to gloss.  This digital edition does implement an interesting vehicle for footnotes; it has a separate window at the bottom that can be navigated independently, either by scrolling or clicking on the note indicators within the text.  However, like with the rest of the page, this is also too small, and becomes more of an annoyance to use than a helpful tool.

#### 1.2 The Piers Plowman Electronic Archive 
This digital edition has some incredible functionality not found in other digital editions, and in many ways does make excellent use of its digital medium.  For example, highlighting any word within the body of the text on any manuscript version pops up a link to the MED (Middle English Dictionary) from the University of Michigan, with the page of results for that particular word.  It attempts in its textual design to mimic the layout and styling of the original manuscript.  It also offers several different views of the same manuscript on the same page, allowing the user to easily view different approaches to the same text.  However, it does have several problems.  For one, while images of the original manuscript are available to view on each of the different manuscript versions, they are placed so unintuitively as to be essentially nonexistent.  Starting at the top of the page, they are inserted as a simple citation notes following the number of the folio page being represented (see the top of the page in figure 1.2.1).  Clicking this, what appears to be a note or citation, then opens the manuscript image in full screen, completely obscuring the digital text beneath. (Figure 1.2.2) Not only does this take the reader out of the experience of reading the text, but it means the reader cannot compare the original text to the much more legible digital version.  Separating the manuscript from the text establishes a barrier between the two that a digital edition should be bridging.  The other main issue with this digital edition in the choices in logo and color choice.  The color scheme is a medium gray, with no other color elements apart from the logo, which is a pale blue; however, the color from the logo is not pulled into any other part of the design.  The logo itself (top left of figure 1.2.1) also has almost nothing to do with the content of the website; the three circles almost certainly represent the three distinct version of the Piers Plowman poem, but the branching nodules inside them would seem to indicate something more scientific and less literary.  Overall, the design is not at all indicative of the content.

#### 1.3 The Siege of Jerusalem Electronic Archive 
This archive was developed by some of the same team that made the Piers Plowman electronic archive, so it is no surprise that it suffers from many of the same issues.  The color key (Figure 1.3.2) has a seemingly random assortment of colors, that not only clash with the general color scheme of forest green and tan, but many of them have such low contrast they are nearly impossible to read; specifically the violet, grey, and aqua (see Figure 1.3.2).  As with the PPEA, it also suffers from an outdated design, with an interface that appears like something made a decade ago, and there are no further details on any sort of current iterative updates.  The image integration is slightly better than that of PPEA, as the images appear next to text, but they are too small to be usable as a comparison tool, and clicking on them enlarges them to a full screen view and obscures the digital text, leading the same issue of dividing the original text from the digital instead of bringing them together.

#### 1.4 The Source of the Issues
with digital editions listed above comes primarily from a lack of resources. If we view digital editions as needing to have a balance of humanities and digital input, it is apparent where the scales have tipped.  Scholars of literature wish to create a digital edition, but do not have the necessary background knowledge or access to the right people who have such knowledge in order to construct a fully functional design and interface.  If they are awarded grants for their projects, the majority of funds is spent in the scholarship side of the equation: that is, the notes, glosses, analysis, rights to manuscript images, etc.
If any resources are put towards the digital, it seems to mostly be in the encoding process, which while time consuming work, is perhaps the least technically challenging part of creating a digital edition.  For a digital edition, there is seemingly very little effort put into the digital side of the project.  There is not a lot of documentation on the development process of these projects, so it is mere speculation as to where and how this decision was made.  Regardless, these projects showcase the need for there to be an easier method for these literary scholars to make clean, modern websites that both serve their purpose as scholars but also their purpose as digital editions.

### One Pager
1.	Project: WordPress Theme for Digital Editions
2.	As of the Spring of 2021, there exists no contemporary template or framework in which humanists can construct their digital editions.  These editions must be constructed from the ground up, which uses up much of a project’s time and resources.  Designing and creating a WordPress theme that specifically addresses all of the current issues with digital editions would allow the average user to create their own digital edition, while alleviating some of the burdens normally associated with creating a fully functional digital critical edition.  This project is to create a basic WordPress theme that can be used as a base model for a digital edition for all levels.   As development of a digital edition platform is a budgetary concern for many digital edition projects, this project is designed to provide a ready to use out-of-the-box digital edition template that can be used as is or can be further developed into a more unique design to suit the needs of the project.
3.	Requirements
   1. A complete WordPress theme
   2. Allows for digital page text to be added to a page
      1. Integrated admin template for adding digital pages
      2. Plugin/custom block for adding line numbers
   3.  Allows for notes and glosses to be added to text
   4.  Allows for images to be uploaded and added to a page
   5.  Cohesive base design that is “comfortably modern”
4. Maybe-Scope (Would Like to Have)
   1. Allow for XML uploads of documents to be auto formatted
   2. Multi-views that can be chosen by the user
5. Out of Scope
   1. Translation services – all documents will need to be provided by user
   2. Draw-on annotation tools
   3. Export/share individual documents

### Personas
For this project there are to separate groups of personas – those who would use the theme to create a digital edition, and those who would view a digital edition.
1. Creators
   1. Scholars – Those with a manuscript, pages, or other work that they wish to create a digital edition for.  These people would be presumed to have little to no experience with coding.
   2. Web Developers – Those who have been hired on by an individual (e.g., lone scholar) or a group (e.g., University Library, etc.) to design the web interface for the digital editions.  These people would have a working knowledge of WordPress, HTML, CSS, and PHP, but may not know the all the details of the specific work being digitized.
   3. Workers – This group may include interns, fellows, or work study recipients who have been hired on to help with the project.  These people would have a range of knowledge when it comes to working with WordPress.
2. Readers
   1. Scholars – Those with an already established interest in the field, author, or their work, and are pursuing more scholarship in those areas.
   2. Researchers – Those who are completing research in a related topic, and for whom a digital edition would be a helpful source of information to aid in their goals.
   3. Students – Those who have been specifically assigned this work.
   4. Lay-people with an interest – People without a direct academic goal who have an interest in the field/author/work.

### Design Brief
The basic layout for the digital edition was made with an eye to making the interface clean and modern.  A digital edition should strike a happy medium between showcasing serious scholarship, and a pleasant user experience that is intuitive to use.

The majority of the main body of the pages on this site are left to be filled with text or images, so I have made the static elements like the navigation and logo as unobtrusive as possible.  The top navigation bar is intentionally small for this reason, but not too small as to be difficult to read.  The menu elements highlight on hover and stay highlighted when the user moves to that page, making for easy navigation.

There will be five main types of pages that can be generated from this theme.  Each of these maintains the same navigation bar at the top:
1. Front page – only three elements are in the main body of the front-page design: a large, full color and (encouraged to be) full resolution image, a large title for the project, and a brief paragraph explanation of the project.  The front page is meant to serve as a welcome and brief introduction – in order to keep the design clean and simple, further details and the project and the development should be delegated to deeper pages.

Descriptive page  – A page that can contain a variety of information, such as the history of the text or project, detailed analysis, etc.  Anything that is not the text itself but is text heavy should use this page design.  It has a large header at the top to describe the contents of the page, then sections topped with sub headers, which serve as anchor points for a side navigation menu, which displays a timeline-like menu system through which the user can navigate up and down the page.  This page will also allow for other types of media integration, such as images, audio, and video.
